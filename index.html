The Business Challenge
Every call has a price tag. We reverse-engineered it.

A prospective acquirer prices every individual loan call with a secret formula — not one score per customer, a fresh value for every call. They revealed their value for 231,146 calls and asked us to predict it for 25,698 new ones, scored on how close our number lands to theirs.

Our Task

Given 231,146 historical calls where the acquirer's VALUE was known, build a model that predicts VALUE for 25,698 new calls — scored on Mean Absolute Error (MAE), the average dollar gap to the true value. We optimised directly for that metric and validated everything on calls the model never saw during training.

Our Analytical Approach
From six messy data sources to one number we can defend

Six raw systems, reconciled into one ledger, then modelled with two specialist regressors — deliberately no black-box neural nets.

1
Data Cleaning
Six raw sources — call logs, customer demographics, employee rosters, loan applications, disbursements and repayment telemetry — reconciled into one 231k-row ledger. All raw PII (SSNs, passport numbers, credit card numbers, plaintext passwords, IPv6 addresses) was stripped before any modelling.
2
Two-World Segmentation
Calls split cleanly into “no loan issued” (44.3% of calls, value ≈$55, highly predictable) vs. “loan issued” (55.7%, value ≈$3,951, all the variation). Each gets its own specialist model — simpler and far more accurate than one combined model.
3
Model Training
Two gradient-boosted regressors (XGBoost, reg:absoluteerror), trained to directly minimise Mean Absolute Error — the exact metric we're scored on. The loan specialist predicts value as a fraction of loan size, which sharpened large-loan estimates considerably.
4
Validation & Fairness Check
Tested on a held-out 20% of calls never seen in training. We then re-trained with every ethically sensitive attribute removed and compared accuracy — see Ethics below for the result.
Value Distribution Across All Training Calls
231,146 calls — sharply bimodal: near-zero for no-loan calls, a long tail for loan calls
Key insight 44% of all calls cluster at $0–100 (no loan issued). The rest spread from a few hundred dollars to tens of thousands — which is exactly why we model the two groups separately instead of forcing one curve to fit both.
Key Findings
What actually drives loan-call value — and what doesn't

The differences come from the loan and how it's repaid — not from who's calling or what they say the money is for.

Acceptance Rate & Average Value by Loan Purpose
Acceptance is flat across purposes — typical loan size is what differs
Management implication Acceptance rate is essentially flat (55–57%) across every loan purpose. What differs is the typical amount requested — that's why “loan purpose” is only a small (5.9%) driver of value, acting entirely through loan size, not approval odds.

Once a loan is issued, credit score barely moves its value
Management implication Average value is essentially flat across credit-score bands — the highest-score band ($3,603) is actually slightly below the middle bands. Credit score doesn't make our model's top-9 driver list at all.

Ethical Framework
Fair by design — and it cost us nothing

A valuation model can quietly discriminate if it leans on personal characteristics. We built it without any sensitive personal data at all, and verified the business loses nothing by doing so.

⚖️
Protected Characteristics in the Raw Data
The dataset includes client race, sex, nativity, age, marital status and number of children — all protected or proxy-protected under fair-lending law (ECOA). Using these in a model that could influence resource or credit decisions would be both illegal and discriminatory, regardless of predictive power.
Safeguard: excluded from every feature used by the deployed (clean) model
🗺️
Geographic Proxies for Race
State, zip code, timezone, urban-vs-rural flag, and zip-code coordinates can act as proxies for race and income (redlining risk), even with no protected attribute present directly.
Safeguard: every geographic identifier removed from the clean model
📊
Does Fairness Cost Accuracy?
We trained the identical model twice on the same held-out split — once with every sensitive attribute included, once without. Held-out MAE: $86.61 with sensitive data vs. $86.29 without. Removing every sensitive attribute didn't cost a cent of accuracy — if anything it was marginally better.
Result: fairness here is effectively free
🔒
Sensitive PII in the Raw Data
The raw dataset contains Social Security numbers, passport numbers, credit card numbers and issuers, plaintext passwords, IPv6 addresses and barcodes — a serious data-governance issue independent of modelling.
Safeguard: all raw PII dropped before any feature engineering
👥
Who Is Affected
Calls that don't convert into a loan skew toward financially comfortable callers — about 53% higher income, stronger credit scores, more likely employed — i.e. people who likely didn't need the loan, not people who were turned away. Caveat: the data shows whether a call converted, not who declined, so we can describe the pattern but not assign the decision to either side.
Safeguard: value is one input to resource decisions, never the sole basis for declining service
📢
Transparency & Governance
The model is fully explainable gradient-boosted trees — no neural networks — so any single prediction can be traced back to the SHAP-based drivers behind it. We recommend regular bias-drift audits and a human in the loop for any decision the score informs.
Safeguard: SHAP attribution available for every prediction

Our Bottom Line on Ethics

This model should inform resource allocation and portfolio analysis — not decide who receives a loan. Built without a single sensitive personal attribute, and proven to lose no accuracy by doing so, it lets management defend every dollar figure to a regulator or partner. Adopt the clean model as standard, and routinely check that no proxy (like geography) sneaks bias back in.

Strategic Recommendations
Four levers to raise value per call, in priority order

Framed so growth comes from customers succeeding, not from charging anyone more.

1
Convert more of the right enquiries into loans
44.3% of all calls end with no loan issued — worth about $55 each. Helping more suitable enquirers find a well-matched loan is the single biggest unlock; it doesn't require relaxing underwriting, just better matching at the point of the call.
Biggest prize · start here
2
Keep good loans performing with early support
A charged-off loan settles around $2,600 — roughly half of a healthy on-time loan's $4,186. Proactive reminders and hardship support before a loan tips into 30–60 day delinquency protect both the customer and the realised value.
High impact · low cost
3
Right-size loans for qualified borrowers
Value scales hard with loan size: $1,322 average value under $1k requested, vs. $21,306 average above $10k. Offering appropriately larger loans to borrowers who can comfortably sustain them grows value responsibly.
Medium term
4
Keep the clean (non-sensitive) model as the production standard
Since removing every sensitive attribute and geographic proxy cost no accuracy, there is no business case for ever reintroducing them. Pair this with a recurring bias-drift audit and a human in the loop on any decision the score touches.
Ongoing discipline
For the Curious
Technical Detail

Heavy methodology kept here — out of the main flow but available for sceptical readers.

What model did we use, and why? ▼

Two specialist XGBoost regressors, both trained with the reg:absoluteerror objective — i.e. optimised directly for Mean Absolute Error, the exact metric we're scored on:

  • A “no-loan” specialist for the 44.3% of calls where no loan was issued — low, steady value, easy to predict.
  • A “loan” specialist for the 55.7% of calls where a loan was issued, trained to predict value as a fraction of loan size (weighted by loan size), then rescaled back to dollars. This sharpened large-loan estimates considerably.

We also benchmarked Random Forest and Ridge regression; XGBoost gave the lowest held-out MAE. We deliberately avoided neural networks — every prediction here is explainable.

How did we handle the two segments and extreme values? ▼

Every call — in training and in the set to be valued — carries a LOAN_id that is present if and only if a loan was issued. We use that to route each call to its specialist:

  1. No LOAN_id → the no-loan specialist predicts value directly.
  2. Has a LOAN_id → the loan specialist predicts value/loan-size, then we multiply back by the requested loan size.
  3. Final predictions are clipped to the minimum and maximum VALUE actually observed in training — a safety bound so the model can never output an impossible number.
